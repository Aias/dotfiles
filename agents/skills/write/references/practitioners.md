# Source Material: Collected Practitioners

Writing insights from working writers, organized thematically.

## On Seeing Clearly

### Henrik Karlsson — Look at the thing, not the words

> When drawing from real life, the trick is to spend more time looking at the thing you are trying to capture than at your drawing. This is true for writing, too.

> Good writing isn't about studying how great essays or poems look and then figuring out how to reverse engineer that (though that's part of it). Rather, it's about learning how to pay close attention to your thoughts and feelings and the concrete materiality of the world, and mastering the craft so you can translate what you see onto the page.

> The most useful piece of writing advice you can squeeze into 3 words is, I think, "Don't think, look!" (Wittgenstein) So much bad writing comes from people moving words about on the page, instead of staring at the real thing and then adjusting their words to fit it.

### Dwarkesh Patel — Just write that

> Unreasonably effective writing advice: "What are you trying to say here? Okay, just write that."

## On Revision

### George Saunders — Micro-decisions

> The way I revise is: I read my own text and imagine a little meter in my head, with "P" on one side ("Positive") and "N" on the other ("Negative")... This involves making thousands of what I've come to think of as "micro-decisions." These are instantaneous, intuitive — I just prefer this to that.

> With each choice, even the smallest ones, the story becomes more and more... well, it becomes more her, you could say. There's more of her essential nature in it, more of what will distinguish her from all of those other writers out there.

### John McPhee — Draft No. 4

> It is toward the end of the second draft, if I'm lucky, when the feeling comes over me that I have something I want to show to other people. After reading the second draft aloud, and going through the piece for the third time (removing the tin horns and radio static that I heard while reading), I enclose things in boxes for Draft No. 4. If I enjoy anything in this process it is Draft No. 4. I go searching for replacements for the words in the boxes.

### Lucy Keer — Three-pass writing

> Pass 1: landmarks. I paste in all the things I already know I want to cover, in roughly the right order. Pass 2: everything else except the annoying bits. Then I draw the rest of the owl, except the absolute worst parts. Pass 3: the annoying bits. A small-scale version of Finishing The Project mode.

## On Logic and Vibe

### Sarah Constantin — Two channels

> The other thing I try to do, which you won't find in the style manuals, is to write for both logic and vibe. Logic: is what I'm trying to say coherent and true? Are my arguments valid? Vibe: are the associations and emotions likely to be evoked by my words the ones I intend?

> You want to avoid saying "not X" where X is the most vivid, high-emotional-intensity word in the sentence. People's attention will be drawn to the vivid word first, and much less to the neutral word "not" denying it.

> There's a level on which you read a passage as a sequence of words structured into clauses, connected by logical, meaning-bearing relationships. And there's also a level that's more like a bag of words — the order doesn't matter, relationship words like "not" and "but" don't matter, some part of the mind is just scanning for vivid words and phrases that evoke associations and memories and feelings. Your writing will be easier to understand and more emotionally resonant if the vibes evoked from a "bag-of-words" skim match the overall point you're trying to make.

### Sarah Constantin — Concision and density

> Most of the reworking I do, at the clause/sentence/paragraph level, is for concision and density. I want the greatest amount of vivid and important stuff, and the least amount of boring stuff, that I can fit in each space without the overall flow sounding unnatural.

## On Grammar and Rhetoric

### George Orwell — Six rules

From *Politics and the English Language*:

1. Never use a metaphor, simile or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

### Henry Oliver — Grammar is logic

> So many people read writing advice... Here's the thing. You should ignore this stuff. You won't ever read the most important thing a writer can know, that grammar is logic. Sound is married to sense.

> Unless the people offering writing advice are going to tell you what antithesis is, or a periodic sentence, and show you different ways of using them, all you are getting is over-generalised, over-simplified, opinion. Good writing advice will tell you the functions of grammar, like what a colon does: in Fowler's words, a colon delivers the goods invoiced in the previous clause.

> However you write, write left-branching sentences. Like detective movies, they open unexpectedly. Nothing could be more apposite, nor more satisfying, than a good old-fashioned periodic sentence.

> You are better off reading books like the various grammar guides, Tufte's *Syntax as Style*, Fowler's *Modern English*, Forsyth's *Elements of Eloquence*. And of course, read as much actual good writing as possible. The best writing advice is actually reading advice.

## On Precision

### Matt Might — Three kinds of weasel words

> Weasel words — phrases or words that sound good without conveying information — obscure precision. I notice three kinds in my students' writing:

(a) **Salt and pepper words** — sprinkled in for seasoning. Look and feel technical, convey nothing: "various", "a number of", "fairly", "quite".

(b) **Beholder words** — meaning is a function of the reader: "interestingly", "surprisingly", "remarkably", "clearly". Peer reviewers don't like judgments drawn for them.

(c) **Lazy words** — avoid making a quantitative characterization: "very", "extremely", "several", "many", "most", "few", "vast".

> The point of these scripts is not to ban all use of constructs like the passive voice. The point is to make sure that my students and I make a conscious choice to use these constructs.

### Maury Maverick — Gobbledygook

> The Naval officer who wired "Sighted Sub — Sank Same" told the whole story. Put the real subject matter — the point — and even the conclusion, in the opening paragraph and the whole story on one page. Period.

> Stay off gobbledygook language. It only fouls people up. For the Lord's sake, be short and say what you're talking about. Anyone using the words "activation" or "implementation" will be shot.

## On Understanding

### Dan Luu — Surfaces versus fundamentals

> Most advice is written by people who don't understand how their writing works. If you watch the median dance instructor and listen to their instructions, you'll see that their instructions are quite different from what they actually do.

> The more general issue is that a person who doesn't understand the thing they're trying to copy will end up copying unimportant superficial aspects of what somebody else is doing and miss the fundamentals that drive the superficial aspects. There isn't a shortcut to understanding something deeply enough to facilitate doing it well that can be summed up in simple rules, like "omit needless words."

### maya.land — Skepticism of beautiful prose

> People tend to underestimate how effective good rhetorical technique can be in shaping our perceptions of writing's underlying arguments. If I notice something's off about what I'm writing, I may end up unintentionally wallpapering over mold. I may take a structural flaw in my argument, misperceive it as a flaw of my expression, and cover it over with a nice turn of phrase.

> I find it good to be extra skeptical of ideas expressed by prose that I like, that feels clear and flowing. On a subconscious level, one probably can't help but overweight arguments in packages one finds appealing.

## On Writing as Thinking

### Henrik Karlsson — The cracks are the point

> When I write, I get to observe the transition from this fluid mode of thinking to the rigid. As I type, I'm often in a fluid mode — writing at the speed of thought. I feel confident about what I'm saying. But as soon as I stop, the thoughts solidify, rigid on the page, and, as I read what I've written, I see cracks spreading through my ideas. What seemed right in my head fell to pieces on the page. Seeing your ideas crumble can be a frustrating experience, but *it is the point* if you are writing to think. You want it to break. It is in the cracks the light shines in.

### Henrik Karlsson — Essays from animating questions

> When I get excited about something, I articulate that as an "animating question." Without that, the material is just a lump, and reading it feels like drifting.

### Zy Mazza — The "in other words" test

> One rule of thumb for more concise writing: If you find yourself saying "in other words, ..." 9 times out of ten you can delete everything that came before it and just use those "other words" in the first place.
